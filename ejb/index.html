Title: Enterprise JavaBeans, 3rd Edition
Author: Richard Monson-Haefel
Publisher O'Reilly & Associates
Publication date: September 2001
ISBN: 0-596-00226-2

Enterprise JavaBeans, by Richard Monson-Haefel, covers versions 2.0 and 1.1 of the Enterprise JavaBeans specification. (Version 1.0, covered in previous editions of the book, has been dropped, which is no great loss.) In an excellent introductory chapter, the book describes the background of EJB as the convergence of two technologies: transaction monitors (such as CICS and TUXEDO); and distibuted object technologies (such as CORBA and Java RMI). Subsequent chapters go on to describe the high-level architecture and primary services (resource management, location transparency, security, transactions, persistence) of EJB. These initial chapters are quite theoretical, but this is as it should be; the ideas that underlie EJB are not trivial, and are unlikely to be well understood simply by diving straight into code examples.

After the initial three chapters, there is plenty of code. The author develops a single example throughout (that of a cruise business), which lends the book a nice consistency. Unfortunately, some of the code examples are rather clumsy (for example, using an exception as a signal to break out of a loop), but this doesn't detract much from the clarity of the ideas being developed.

Arguably the core of the book is its extremely comprehensive coverage of EJB 2.0's container-managed persistence (CMP), to which three substantial chapters are devoted. This is not overkill; it's a complex topic, and the detailed coverage is much needed. There is also a single chapter on EJB 1.1's much simpler version of CMP.

The book devotes an entire chapter to the subject of transactions, again an abstract and difficult topic that is presented with clarity.

Message-driven beans, the other major addition to EJB 2.0 along with the new CMP model, is also dealt with thoroughly. (The author has also co-authored another book on the Java Message Service, the technology that underlies message-driven beans.)

Although the book does not attempt to cover advanced design strategies (rightly so, as this topic would take several books by itself), a chapter on Design Strategies explains a few of the most important techniques such as value objects and business interfaces.

Finally, there is a detailed chapter on XML deployment descriptor files, and another that explains how EJBs fit into the "big picture" of the Java 2, Enterprise Edition platform.

Overall, this is an excellent book for anyone with solid Java skills and a working knowledge of SQL, who wants to acquire a thorough understanding of Enterprise JavaBeans.
