Title: Java Message Service
Author: Richard Monson-Haefel & David A. Chappell
Publisher O'Reilly & Associates
Publication date: December 2000
ISBN: 0-596-00068-5

Although the book Java Message Service is only 238 pages in length, relatively short for an O'Reilly Java title, it nevertheless provides a fairly comprehensive introduction to the standard Java API for message-oriented middleware, co-written by the author of Enterprise JavaBeans and the lead engineer for Progress Software's SonicMQ messaging product.

Chapter 1 provides a brief, but useful, introduction to the concept of enterprise message, explaining the role of messaging middleware, and contrasting it with synchronous communication mechanisms sich as RMI. It outlines different messaging architectures, and introduces the Java Message Service and key concepts such as clients, providers, producers and consumers. It also describes the two JMS messaging domains—publish-and-subscribe (pub/sub) and point-to-point (p2p)—which are described more fully in chapters 4 and 5 respectively. Chapter 2 develops a simple, illustrative application using JMS.

Chapter 3 describes in detail the structure of a JMS message, including headers and properties, and also describes the selector mechanism that can be used to filter incoming messages. It also describes the various specialized message types: TextMessage, ObjectMessage, BytesMessage, StreamMessage and MapMessage.

Chapters 4 and 5 present detailed examples using the the pub/sub and p2p messaing domains respectively. Since the APIs for the two domains are very similar, the material in these two chapters is somewhat repetetive.

Chapters 6 and 7 delve into more advanced topics. Chapter 6 covers guaranteed messaging and the various acknowledgement modes (AUTO_ACKNOWLEDGE, DUPS_OK_ACKNOWLEDGE and CLIENT_ACKNOWLEDGE). It also covers the important subject of JMS transactions, and the role of both local and distributed transactions, as well as failure conditions. Chapter 7 discusses deployment considerations, and in particular contains useful advice on testing JMS providers to ensure that they can meet non-functional requirements for performance, scalability and reliability.

Chapter 8 provides a very brief overview of the role of JMS in the J2EE platform, and in particular its relationship to message-driven beans. The final chapter provides an overview of JMS implementations—both commercial and open-source—although this material is probably too dated to be of much value.

Overall, the book provides good coverage of the APIs and is useful as an introductory tutorial. However, there is little discussion of design issues in enterprise applications. Also, the examples are somewhat basic. For example, there is no discussion of using JMS for XML messaging; while this would obviously go beyond the core JMS APIs, it is such a common scenario in practice that more coverage would be useful.
